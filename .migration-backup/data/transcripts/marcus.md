# User Research: Marcus Holland, GM and Co-owner, The Crescent

**Interviewer:** Anil (Greenroom PM)
**Date:** Late January 2026, 25 minutes, in-person at The Crescent
**Marcus is one of three co-owners. He runs the venue's business side: lease, payroll, sponsor relationships, the books.**

---

**Anil:** Thanks for the time. I want to understand settlement from the business side. What does it look like to you?

**Marcus:** I'm the last signature on every settlement before money moves. Mariana texts me a screenshot of her final number, I review it, I send back "ok" or sometimes I have a question, then she wires it on Monday. So in any given week I'm signing off on five to seven settlements, almost always at 1 or 2am.

**Anil:** How long does it take you to review one?

**Marcus:** Honestly, between 30 seconds and ten minutes. Most of them, 30 seconds. I trust Mariana. If the number looks like it's in the range I'd expect for that show — the deal terms I already know, the gross I can ballpark from the night's ticket sales which I follow — I just sign off.

The ten-minute ones are the ones where the number looks weird or where I know we had a complicated deal.

**Anil:** And how often does signing off at 2am turn out to have been wrong later?

**Marcus:** [laughs] Once or twice a year, badly. We had the marketing recoup thing last March that cost us $1,200. We had a hospitality dispute the year before that cost us $400. There's a long tail of $50-$100 things that get caught in reconciliation the next week and quietly resolved.

**Anil:** What's the actual cost of those mistakes?

**Marcus:** Two costs. One is the actual dollar concession — that's small at our scale. Two, much bigger, is the reputation cost with the agency. WME, CAA, the bigger agencies are routing 50+ shows a year through cities like Nashville. If we develop a reputation as the venue that has settlement issues, we lose the deals we want most. We won't be told we're being routed around — they'll just stop calling.

**Anil:** Have you seen evidence of that happening?

**Marcus:** Yeah. Three years ago there was an indie agent — I won't name them — who had a bad settlement experience here. We thought it was resolved. Eighteen months later I noticed all of their roster had stopped routing through us. I called the agent and asked. She told me, very nicely, that she'd been routing through The Basement for those tours instead. That was about $80K in gross revenue we lost over a year.

So when I think about the cost of a bad settlement, the dollars on the night are the smallest part. The bigger thing is whether we lose the relationship.

**Anil:** Tell me about the lease.

**Marcus:** Our lease is up in March 2027. The landlord's been reasonable — we've been here since 2009, they like us — but the renewal is going to be at a higher rate. Whether we can absorb that increase comes down to whether we're filling the room. Whether we're filling the room comes down to whether agents are routing us strong shows. Whether agents route us strong shows comes down to a lot of things, but settlement is one of them.

So when Pri at Greenroom — your CEO — says settlement matters, I'm right there with her. This is the most important thing your product touches at our venue.

**Anil:** What would change for you if settlement worked materially better?

**Marcus:** A few things. One: I could spend less of my time and Mariana's time on it. Right now we collectively spend probably 25 hours a month on settlement and post-settlement cleanup. If that were five hours a month, that's 20 hours of senior labor back into booking, marketing, and sponsor work — which are the levers that grow revenue.

Two: I'd sleep better. I sign off on settlements via text from my couch, half-asleep, on basically blind faith. If the system gave me real visibility into what I was signing — flagged anything unusual — I'd be more confident.

Three: we'd probably have better margin variance. Right now our predicted vs actual margin diverges 40%+ on half of shows, which we don't fully understand. Some of that is settlement. The deals are complex, the expenses are sometimes wrong, the math has hidden absorbed costs.

**Anil:** What's the relationship between Greenroom and your work?

**Marcus:** I don't use Greenroom much directly. I look at the dashboard maybe once a week. My main interaction with it is reading Mariana's settlement screenshots. Honestly, I'm probably the wrong person to ask about features — Mariana's the user.

But I will say this. If your product helps Mariana settle cleaner, that helps us protect the lease. If it helps her get better at managing agent relationships, that helps us protect the lease. The CEO's memo got it right. We don't need more features. We need the ones that touch trust to actually work.

**Anil:** Last question — anything you wish your booker could do that they can't today?

**Marcus:** I wish she could see, before a show even happens, whether the deal we agreed to is going to be a clean one or a messy one. Right now we find out at 2am. By then it's too late to fix anything. If we could see Wednesday "this deal is going to have an ambiguous expense fight" we could resolve it cold, in writing, with the agent. The way settlement works now is we're paying a tax on every poorly-written deal email we ever signed.

**Anil:** Got it. Thank you, Marcus.

**Marcus:** Sure. Send me the prototype when you have it.
