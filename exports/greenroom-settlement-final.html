Why simplification, not more technology

The intuitive response to a broken settlement tool is to build a better one — a smarter calculator, an AI that reads deal notes, an agentic workflow that asks the booker the right questions. We considered this. We chose not to build it.

The reason is in the data and in the transcripts. The problem at The Crescent is not that Mariana lacks a tool that can calculate a vs deal. She built one — it's her Google Sheet, and it works. The problem is that the underlying deal structures are inconsistent, the expense data lives in four places, and the deal terms are written in prose that two people read differently at midnight. A more powerful tool built on top of that substrate would inherit every one of those problems.

The right move is to fix two specific, high-impact structural problems completely — door deals that lose money by construction, and $1–5K deals that carry dispute friction without changing the payout. Solve each one well. Generate clean data. Let that foundation carry the next layer.

What I cut and why

These are deliberate choices. Each represents a direction that looks plausible on the surface and fails on closer examination — either because it solves the wrong layer of the problem, or because it requires clean input data that doesn't exist yet.

Cut
A universal Vs deal calculator
Build the in-app calculator Mariana needs for vs deals, % of net, door deals, walkout pots, tier ratchets. Cut because it solves the wrong problem. Mariana already has a calculator — her spreadsheet, and it works. Disputes don't happen because the math is wrong. They happen because deal terms are ambiguous and expense data is scattered. A better in-app calculator inherits both problems unchanged. The data confirms it: dispute rates correlate with deal structure complexity, not with which tool performed the arithmetic.
Cut
An AI that reads deal notes and extracts terms
An LLM that parses Mariana's freetext deal notes and populates structured fields. Cut because it automates the wrong layer. The freetext notes exist because the structured fields don't model real deals. An AI reading ambiguous prose doesn't eliminate the ambiguity — it hides it one level deeper. When the model misreads a recoup clause, you get the same $720 dispute the Coastal Spell show produced, now with an AI-generated number attached.
Cut
$5–15K deals in this proposal
The highest dispute rate in the data at 13.5%. Cut for two reasons. First, deal complexity at this level is genuinely different — more negotiated exceptions, more agent leverage, more multi-party communication that requires human judgment. A suggestion engine cannot safely automate this yet. Second, the sample is 37 shows — too thin for reliable cell-mean suggestions. This is the next research sprint, not this one.
Cut
Agentic workflows and automated advancing
An AI that walks Mariana through deal checklists, sends structured deal confirmations, and flags ambiguities automatically. Cut because agentic tools require clean input data to produce trustworthy output. With non-standard contracts and prose deal notes as the substrate, the system either flags everything (noise) or misses the edge cases that cause disputes (false confidence). You need simplified deal structures before agentic tools can be reliable on top of them. That is what Phases 1 and 2 produce.

Phase 1 — Door deals

Phase 1 · MVP · Immediate Replace the door deal structure with a hybrid 29 shows · 24 months · $0–1K bracket
Problem

Under the current door arrangement, all ticket revenue above production expenses goes to the artist. The venue absorbs all costs and receives nothing from the show regardless of how it performs. A sold-out night and a half-empty night produce the same result for the venue: zero net. This is not a pricing problem or a negotiation problem — it is a structural one. The deal is designed so the venue cannot win.

Aggregate venue net across all 29 door deals in the last 24 months is −$29,056, an average of −$1,007 per show. These are not edge-case losses. They are the expected outcome of a deal structure that gives the venue no participation in the upside.

Finding

27 of 29 door deals (93%) left the venue with a net loss. No other deal type in any gross bracket comes close. Pale Lake is the clearest example: on a sold-out night with $19,296 in gross receipts, the door structure paid the artist $17,387 and left the venue with exactly $0.

The MVP action

At deal entry, Greenroom proposes a hybrid structure: $500 artist floor + 60/40 revenue split above a $1,500 expense cap. Both parties see the projected outcome at the cell-mean gross of $4,420 before committing. The proposal is precise and grounded — not a general suggestion to "negotiate differently," but a specific pre-calculated alternative that replaces a deal structure that loses money 93% of the time.

For high-gross shows where an artist or agent negotiates different terms, the venue adjusts the contract manually. Exceptions at this volume are rare enough to handle case by case — they do not require a separate workflow. The 10 structurally unviable shows below $2,223 gross are identified at booking, not at 2am.

This is a contained, complete intervention on a specific deal type. The goal is not to solve all door deal complexity in one release — it is to solve the 27 shows that are losing money by construction, solve them well, and generate the clean data that informs what comes next.

Expected outcome

The 29-show analytics picture — 27 of 29 losing money, −$29,056 aggregate venue net — establishes the structural case for replacing door deals entirely. Smart Switch's projected grid, which applies stricter eligibility (settled shows with all pre-show inputs available and a valid suggestion), currently covers 6 of those 29 deals, projecting $13,211 in venue-side savings at an average of ~$2,200 per converted show — the highest per-deal savings of any deal type. The remaining 23 shows are the structural target: as more door deals are renegotiated to hybrid terms and accumulate clean data, they grow into Smart Switch eligibility. The door deal dispute rate drops to near zero for converted shows — pre-agreed hybrid terms eliminate the recoup arithmetic that generates every dispute in this cell.

Phase 2 — $1–5K vs and % of net deals

Phase 2 · MVP · Short-term Convert vs and % of net deals to pre-agreed flat guarantees 137 vs · 102 % of net · 24 months
Problem

Vs deals and % of net deals exist to protect the artist when a show significantly overperforms — when the percentage would produce more than the guarantee. That protection has real value at higher gross levels. At the $1–5K bracket, the expense structure at this venue consumes the available pool before the percentage produces a meaningfully different outcome from a flat. The complexity remains, but the economic reason for it rarely materialises.

The cost of that complexity shows up in dispute rates. This is the highest-volume deal bracket at The Crescent — 241 shows in 24 months — and vs deals within it dispute at 12.4%, more than three times the rate of flat deals in the same bracket on similar economics. Smart Switch's projected grid, applying strict pre-show eligibility, covers 34 of the 137 vs / $1–5K deals — the actionable sub-set where a valid flat suggestion was available before show night. Across those 34 deals, the model projects $9,186 in venue-side savings and approximately 5 fewer disputes (70% reduction to the flat baseline of 3.8%). The wider 137-deal cell remains the analytical base that establishes the structural case.

Every settlement-flow attention rule in the system — disputed recoups, status-versus-notes mismatches, stale disputes — traces back to the recoup arithmetic that complex deal structures generate. A pre-agreed flat removes the largest share of that arithmetic.

An honest note on the economics: The percentage clause on vs deals does fire — across the 24-month history, 116 of 133 settled vs deals (87.2%) had the artist's percentage out-pay the guarantee, with a mean overshoot of ~$2,338 per show and the remaining 12.8% paid at the guarantee floor (avg guarantee-win $0). The case for pre-agreed flats is not that the clause produces nothing. It is that the dispute cost (12.4% vs 3.8%) outweighs the complexity on shows where the flat and vs terms produce similar outcomes. SGP's suggested flat accounts for this by picking whichever side of the deal the artist would have won and proposing that as the flat — so the conversion is calibrated to the actual historical payout, not a discount from it.
The MVP action

At deal entry, Greenroom runs Smart Guaranteed Pricing (SGP) — a seven-step deterministic waterfall — to produce a suggested flat guarantee for the show. SGP is not a lookup of the cell average. It reads the deal's structure, resolves expected gross from a source waterfall (artist history at this venue → artist history anywhere → agent history → cell mean → venue mean → capacity proxy), estimates capped expenses from comparable shows, projects what the percentage clause would have paid, and proposes the higher of the two — rounded to the nearest $50. The suggested flat is always ≥ the contract guarantee, so converting never underpays the artist versus their signed number. Every input and step is persisted in an audit record the booker can share with the agent.

Shows in draft, submitted, or in-review status are flagged automatically when an A or B tier suggestion is available — the existing settlement lifecycle routes the proposal to the agent for confirmation. No new infrastructure required. When the agent accepts, the deal converts to a clean flat in the database carrying the full SGP audit trail. The AI presents the proposal conversationally: "Based on how similar shows have settled here, a flat of $5,600 reflects what the vs terms would have produced on this night — the $4,000 guarantee was the floor; the percentage would have paid $5,610. Want to offer that now?" That is a Wednesday email, not a midnight calculation.

This intervention is narrow by design. It targets the $1–5K bracket specifically — the data supports reliable suggestions here, the volume is high enough to matter, and the dispute rate is high enough to make a visible difference quickly. It does not attempt to address $5–15K deals, walkout pots, or tier ratchets.

Expected outcome

For accepted conversions, settlement requires no calculation — the agreed flat is the payout. The 12.4% dispute rate drops to approximately 3.8% on converted shows — the flat deal baseline in this bracket. The 34 immediately eligible deals are the first batch; as each converted show generates clean structured data, Smart Switch eligibility expands and the savings compound. The data quality problem corrects itself from the inside — each clean deal makes the next suggestion more reliable without a separate data-cleaning step.

How we validate

Twenty-four months of historical backtesting gives confidence in the direction. It does not tell us whether agents will accept the framing, whether the flat-deal suggestion feels fair in practice, or whether the accuracy holds on live shows. Both quantitative and qualitative validation are needed before scaling.

The one thing backtesting cannot validate: agent willingness to pre-commit to a flat guarantee weeks before show night. This is a trust and relationship question as much as a product question. The qualitative readouts are designed to surface friction before it reaches production. Phase 3's protection layer is designed to remove the financial downside that creates hesitation.

Phase 3 — Settlement protection Optional

Phase 3 is optional and independent of Phases 1 and 2. The deal simplification and dispute reduction in the MVP deliver their full value whether or not protection is ever added. Phase 3 is a confidence layer — it exists to accelerate agent adoption of pre-agreed flats by removing the financial uncertainty that makes some agents hesitate. Venues and agents who are comfortable with the simplified structures can skip it entirely.
Phase 3 · Post-MVP · Optional Settlement protection — tiered and packaged Selected clients · beta after MVP
Approach

After Phases 1 and 2 ship, protection is tested with a selected group of client venues and agents. The beta begins with the product that has the highest expected usage — Instant Dispute Pay, sized against The Crescent's actual dispute data: 22 of 503 settlements (4.37%) disputed, averaging $345 per disputed settlement. Suggestion Accuracy Pay is tested in parallel with the same beta cohort.

The full protection program is designed as a tiered offering — different coverage levels, packages, and deductibles that suit different venue sizes, deal volumes, and risk tolerances. Beta learnings from the selected client group shape which tiers make it to general availability, what the caps and pricing look like at scale, and where the partner model versus platform credit model performs better.

Two products · both tested in beta
Product 1 · Highest expected usage
Instant Dispute Pay

When a settlement enters disputed status, the resolution amount is paid immediately — no back-and-forth, no waiting for an email thread to resolve. The dispute is closed; recovery happens in the next billing cycle.

Designed for venues and agents who accept pre-agreed flat deals but want a backstop if a recoup line or expense interpretation is later challenged. Highest expected uptake because the trigger is clear and the value is immediate.

Product 2 · Parallel beta
Suggestion Accuracy Pay

When the accepted flat suggestion differs from what the artist would have earned under the original deal by more than $50, the gap is covered up to a tiered cap. Caps are set from the live SGP backtest (12-month, n=156): median gap $870, p75 $1,569 — priced as three coverage tiers rather than a single fixed amount.

Targets agents who are comfortable with the flat framing but want assurance that the suggestion is accurate before committing weeks in advance.

Delivery model

Both products can be delivered through two mechanisms, and the beta will test which performs better in practice:

Insurance partner model — Greenroom distributes a licensed product through an embedded insurance infrastructure partner (Boost Insurance or Cover Genius, both purpose-built for B2B SaaS platforms via API). The partner underwrites and carries all regulatory and capital requirements. Greenroom earns a 15–20% distribution fee. No licensing overhead, no balance sheet exposure. This is the preferred model for full commercial launch.

Greenroom platform credit — where regulation permits and claim amounts are small, Greenroom resolves disputes directly via platform credit or cash payment. Simpler to deploy in beta, no partner dependency, but carries balance sheet exposure at scale. Used selectively during beta to move fast with selected clients before the partner integration is complete.

Beta findings determine which model carries which product tier into general availability. The two are not mutually exclusive — a tiered program could use the partner model for larger claims and platform credit for smaller ones.

Identified features — not in scope for this proposal

The following features were identified through research and data analysis as delivering real value. They are not included in this proposal — not because they are unimportant, but because each requires the clean data foundation that Phases 1 and 2 produce, and because attempting to build them alongside the structural fix would dilute the MVP's focus. They are listed here so the thinking is visible and the decision can be revisited once the MVP is established.

Expense readiness tracker

What it is: A per-show expense checklist Mariana and her PM populate during the week. CC fees auto-filled from POS. Hospitality flagged amber if rider cap exists but no amount entered by Wednesday.

Why not now: High operational value but requires POS integration and expense data standardisation. That standardisation happens naturally as flat deals accumulate — expenses become simpler to track when there is no recoup arithmetic to resolve against them. Better to build this on clean data than on the current messy baseline. Mariana: "If you could just have all the expenses ready when I sat down to settle, that alone would change my life."

Tour manager preview link

What it is: A read-only, mobile-optimised settlement draft sent to the tour manager during load-out — before the back-office sit-down. Diego reviews the numbers on his phone. He arrives with specific questions, not a cold read. Same link optionally CCs the agent.

Why not now: For flat deals this is almost trivially simple — the preview is a single number. The feature earns its complexity when the settlement involves itemised expenses that the TM needs to review. Build once expense readiness is solved and the settlement statement is consistently complete before the show ends. Diego: "If I could pre-review, the conversation would be 10 minutes instead of 45."

Line-item acknowledgment + audit trail

What it is: At sign-off, each expense line is tagged — standard / absorbed by venue / discussed at table. The signed record captures what both parties confirmed, not just the dollar total. Every line is traceable; no agreement relies on short-term memory.

Why not now: The Coastal Spell dispute was a recoup interpretation fight — exactly what this feature prevents. But for flat deals, there is nothing to acknowledge beyond the agreed amount. This feature's value scales with deal complexity. Prioritise it when the $5–15K segment is addressed, where disputes are frequent and expensive enough to justify a formal acknowledgment layer. Mariana: "There's no record of what the tour manager said yes to."

$5–15K segment

What it is: The highest dispute rate in the data at 13.5%. Structural intervention — potentially a more sophisticated version of the flat suggestion, or a structured deal-term confirmation flow — for shows in the $5–15K guarantee bracket.

Why not now: Deal complexity at this level still requires human back-and-forth that a cell-mean suggestion cannot safely replace. Sample size is 37 shows — insufficient for reliable suggestions. The right next step is a dedicated research sprint on how high-guarantee shows are negotiated, what the dispute triggers actually are, and what a second venue's data shows. This is a separate investigation, not a follow-on feature from this MVP.

The sequencing logic applies here too. Each of these features works better on a foundation of clean, structured deal data than on top of the current messy baseline. Phases 1 and 2 create that foundation. Building these features after the MVP means building them correctly, not twice.
