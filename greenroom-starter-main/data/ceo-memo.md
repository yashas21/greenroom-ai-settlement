# Q4 All-Hands: Where We Are, And Where We're Not

**From:** Pri Shankar
**To:** Greenroom team
**Date:** January 2026

Team,

We just closed our best quarter ever on the topline. ARR is at $8.1M, up from $6.4M when we kicked off the year. We added 73 net new venues, mostly in Texas, Colorado, and the Pacific Northwest. Net dollar retention is 116%. By every dashboard metric the board sees, we're winning.

I want to spend this memo talking about why I'm not satisfied.

When I do my customer calls — and I do five of them a week, usually with bookers, sometimes with GMs — there's a pattern. They love us. They specifically love that they can run their whole operation on Greenroom. Calendar, advances, marketing, settlements, sponsor reporting, fan database — all in one place. The pitch works. Three years in, the comprehensive-platform thesis was right.

But every single one of them says some version of the same thing next: "the individual features are kinda just okay."

The booker at a venue in Portland told me last week that she does all her settlements in a Google Sheet because our settlement tool doesn't handle vs deals. The talent buyer in Asheville said our advance workspace is "better than email but worse than my old PDF system." The GM at a Boston venue, who I know likes us a lot, told me he doesn't trust our show-margin reports enough to share them with his lender.

This is fixable, but I want us to be honest about what it means.

**We are winning on completeness and losing on craft.**

I think this is the most important sentence I'll write this quarter. Our customers love us in spite of our individual features, not because of them. Some of them love us enough to put up with it. The ones who leave us are the ones who hit a feature they care about and find it half-built.

The market that's going to survive the next decade is independent venues with the strongest artist relationships. Live Nation is buying every venue they can. AEG is right behind them. The independents who hold on are going to be the ones agents trust to settle cleanly, fill the room consistently, and treat artists well. Our job is to be the operational backbone of that.

We are not yet good enough at the most important moment of those relationships. Specifically:

**Settlement.** This is where we are most clearly failing. Eighteen percent of our customer base actively uses the in-app settlement tool. The other 82% — including most of our largest accounts — default to spreadsheets. The math gets done somewhere else. The 2am ritual happens somewhere else. The trust between the venue and the artist is built somewhere else. We're not in that conversation. We have to be.

**Advance documents.** Every show generates a five-to-fifteen-page PDF advance from the touring artist. Our workflow is that the booker manually transcribes the relevant bits into our free-text fields and forwards pages by email. We've taken a problem that should be a structured handoff and made it slightly worse than email.

**Sponsor reporting.** Independent venues live or die on sponsor revenue. Bookers spend 6-8 hours per sponsor per quarter manually pulling data out of our system and three other systems to build a Powerpoint. We could be helping. We're not.

I'm not asking the team to fix all three of these in Q1. I'm asking us to pick where to start, go deep, and be honest about the trade-offs we're making.

For Q1, I want our craft bet to be **settlement.** Two reasons. One: it's the most trust-critical moment in the relationship between our customers and their customers' customers. Two: 82% of our base is going around our product to do this. That's not a feature gap. That's an existential signal.

Anil and the product team will be opening up case studies for senior PM hires this quarter, and the work they ship will inform what we build next. If you have strong opinions about what good settlement looks like, talk to them.

We have built an impressive amount of stuff. Now let's build a few things that actually feel good to use.

— Pri
