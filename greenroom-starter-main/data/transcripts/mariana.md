# User Research: Mariana Reyes, Lead Booker, The Crescent

**Interviewer:** Anil (Greenroom PM)
**Date:** Late January 2026, 30 minutes, video call
**Conducted as part of the settlement-deep-dive research sprint.**

This is a lightly cleaned-up transcript. Filler words removed; substantive content preserved.

---

**Anil:** Thanks for making the time. I want to spend most of this on settlement, but maybe start broader — how would you describe what you do at The Crescent?

**Mariana:** I'm the lead booker, which is sort of a misleading title because I do everything that touches the artist relationship. I book the shows, negotiate the deals, advance them, run them on show night, settle them, and follow up the next week. The actual booking — picking who plays — is maybe 20% of my time. The other 80% is operations.

**Anil:** And you've been doing this for —

**Mariana:** Nine years here. Six in this role. I started at the door, then box office, then assistant booker. I'm pretty sure I'm the only person here who knows where everything is.

**Anil:** Walk me through a settlement. Like, what does Friday night actually look like.

**Mariana:** Okay. So the show ends, usually around 11. Crowd's out by midnight. Sound and lights are packing down. Artists are loading out, doing meet-and-greets, that kind of thing. I'm doing a hundred things at once because the bar is closing out and I need to make sure the green room is cleaned and the production manager and I are usually both troubleshooting whatever broke that night. Around midnight or 12:30 the tour manager comes find me — they're either the artist's road manager or sometimes a band member with the spreadsheet eyes — and we go to the back office. I open my laptop. They open theirs. We start.

**Anil:** What's open on your laptop?

**Mariana:** Two tabs. Greenroom — your product — for the deal terms and ticket sales. And a Google Sheet that I started using maybe four years ago that has all the math built in. I copy-paste the numbers from Greenroom into the sheet, type in the expenses, and the sheet does the math.

**Anil:** Why not just do it in our app?

**Mariana:** [pause] You really want me to be honest?

**Anil:** Please.

**Mariana:** Your settlement tool is great if I have a flat-guarantee deal. Or if I'm doing a really simple percentage. Like, "the artist gets 80% of gross, no expenses." Those work fine. But probably 70% of my deals at this venue are vs deals — guarantee versus a percentage of net after expenses. Your tool can't do those. So I tried using it for a while in 2023 and I'd hit a deal it couldn't handle and have to switch to the spreadsheet anyway, which meant I was doing the work twice. So now I just do it in the sheet.

**Anil:** What would it take for you to switch back?

**Mariana:** Honestly? It would have to handle vs deals and bonus tiers correctly, and it would have to feel like I trusted it with the math. Right now if I run a settlement in the in-app tool I have no real visibility into how it got to the answer. The spreadsheet I built has every line called out — I can show the tour manager exactly where each number came from. They can see it. They sign off on it.

**Anil:** Tell me about the conversation with the tour manager.

**Mariana:** Yeah. So the actual settlement is a *conversation*, not a calculation. The math is the easy part. The hard part is the back-and-forth. They'll point at hospitality and say "wait, what's that, that's over the cap" and I have to explain that yes, the whiskey ran over but I'm absorbing the difference. Or they'll argue that one of the production charges shouldn't have been their cost. Or they'll question why CC fees are coming out of net and not gross. Sometimes the conversation is fast — five minutes. Sometimes it's an hour.

**Anil:** What makes the long conversations long?

**Mariana:** Two things. One: the deal terms themselves are ambiguous. We had this dispute last March, I think Marcus told you about it — there was a marketing recoup thing where me and the WME agent disagreed on whether the recoup was inside or outside the expense cap. That cost us $720 in the end and a lot of agent goodwill. The deal email was just unclear and there was no source of truth.

Two: the tour manager doesn't trust the numbers because we don't show our work. Every line in my spreadsheet has a sourceable breakdown. The expenses tie to specific receipts. The ticket sales are reconcilable to the POS. If I just typed in a final number, they wouldn't trust it. The settlement is half about the money, half about the proof.

**Anil:** Got it. After the tour manager signs off, what happens?

**Mariana:** I text Marcus, the GM. I send him a screenshot of the final number. He texts back "ok" or "looks good" within a few minutes. Funds wire on Monday. Then the next morning I take the spreadsheet, export to PDF, and email it to the agent. That's where things sometimes go sideways — the agent reads it carefully and finds something they think is off, and we end up with a dispute thread.

**Anil:** And how often does that happen?

**Mariana:** Some flavor of pushback on maybe 40% of settlements. Most of them resolve fast — the tour manager already signed off so the agent's pushback usually means I forgot to flag something to the artist on the night, or the agent has a different understanding of one term. Maybe one in twenty escalates to a real fight. One or two a year escalates to a check-writing situation.

**Anil:** What do you wish you had?

**Mariana:** Honestly? I want fewer surprises in that 2am conversation. Most of the friction comes from things that were knowable on Wednesday — the hospitality bill is going to run over the rider cap, or the marketing line we agreed to doesn't have a clear deduction order, or the deal email from the agent has a sentence that could mean two different things. If I knew those things on Wednesday I'd email the agent then. The Friday-night version of that conversation goes much worse than the Wednesday-afternoon version.

I want a paper trail. Right now there's literally no record of what happened in the room when we settled. The tour manager points at hospitality, I explain, we agree. That conversation isn't in any system. If we had a dispute later there's no record of what the tour manager actually said yes to. We're operating on trust and short-term memory.

I want the math to be readable to the agent the next morning. Right now I email them a PDF that's basically a stylized version of my spreadsheet. Some agents skim it, some agents read it carefully, some agents have their own template they want me to fill out. There's no consistency.

**Anil:** Last question — where do you spend the most time on settlement that you wish you didn't?

**Mariana:** Pulling expenses together. The CC fees are in Greenroom. The bar charges are in the POS. The hospitality is in receipts that the production manager throws on my desk. The sound and lights are pre-set deal terms but sometimes the engineer adds something. The marketing is wherever I logged it that week. Half my Wednesday is just chasing down expenses for shows that are going to settle on Friday, because if I don't have them ready I'll be doing it at 2am.

If you could just have all the expenses ready when I sat down to settle, that alone would change my life.

**Anil:** Useful. Thank you.

**Mariana:** Thanks. And honestly — please fix this. I've been doing it the same way for six years and I love this job but the 2am ritual is the worst part of every week.

[end transcript]
