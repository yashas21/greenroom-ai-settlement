# User Research: Sarah Kim, Music Agent, WME

**Interviewer:** Anil (Greenroom PM)
**Date:** Late January 2026, 22 minutes, video call
**Sarah books ~80 shows/year for indie acts on her roster. The Crescent is one of the venues she routes through regularly.**

---

**Anil:** Thanks for making time. I want to understand settlement from the agency side. From your seat, what does a "good" venue look like, what does a "bad" one look like?

**Sarah:** Good and bad come down to one thing: do I trust the math.

A good venue is one where my tour manager comes off the road, sends me the settlement statement the next morning, and I can read it in three minutes and not have any questions. The numbers are clear, the deductions are itemized, the deal terms are reflected in the math, and the absorbed-vs-passed-through stuff is called out. I look at it, I confirm with the artist's manager, we move on.

A bad venue is one where I read the settlement and I have questions. Maybe the marketing recoup is buried in expenses. Maybe the percentage is right but the basis it was applied to looks wrong. Maybe there's a line item I don't recognize. When I have to email the booker to ask "what is this," that's already a failure. We've already lost some trust.

**Anil:** How often does that happen?

**Sarah:** I'd say maybe a quarter of settlements I read have something I want to ask about. Maybe one in ten escalates to a back-and-forth that lasts more than a day. One in 30 turns into a real dispute.

**Anil:** What makes a real dispute?

**Sarah:** Money the artist thinks they're owed and the venue thinks they're not. Almost always rooted in a deal-term ambiguity, sometimes in a math error.

**Anil:** Walk me through one.

**Sarah:** Sure. Last March we had one with The Crescent, actually. Coastal Spell, one of my acts. The deal was a vs deal with an expense cap and a marketing recoup line. The booker — Mariana, who I generally like — interpreted the recoup as separate from the expense cap. My read on the deal email was that it was inside the cap. The deal email was written by my colleague Andrea and to be totally honest, it was 80 words long and ambiguous. We resolved it — Marcus the GM made it right — but it was a bad few days for everyone.

The thing that bothered me about that one wasn't even the money. It was that there was no canonical version of what the deal was. Mariana had her notes, I had my email, Andrea had her recollection of the negotiation, and none of them perfectly agreed. The deal was a ghost.

**Anil:** What would have prevented that one?

**Sarah:** A version of the deal we both agreed on, in one place, that had unambiguous terms. The deal email needed three more sentences to be unambiguous. They never got written because deal emails get written at 11pm by overworked agents. So the ambiguity gets pushed downstream into settlement, where it costs everyone more time.

**Anil:** Do you keep a list of venues you trust?

**Sarah:** I do. It's mostly in my head, but I keep notes. There are venues I route hard, venues I route reluctantly, and venues I avoid. Settlement is one of three or four signals I use.

**Anil:** What are the others?

**Sarah:** Whether the room actually fills. Whether the venue treats my artists well — green room, hospitality, sound. Whether the day-of operations run smoothly. And settlement. Settlement isn't the most important — getting the room filled is the most important — but it's the most signal-rich. A venue that settles well has its operational house in order. A venue that settles badly is usually a mess in other ways too.

**Anil:** When you read a settlement statement, what makes it good?

**Sarah:** Three things. Itemization. Provenance. Tone.

Itemization: every deduction is its own line. Not a "miscellaneous expenses: $1,800" — I want to see the sound, the lights, the hospitality, the marketing, the production, broken out.

Provenance: I want to be able to trace each line to a source. The CC fees should match the POS. The expenses should tie to actual receipts I could ask for. I don't actually ask for the receipts most of the time, but I want to know they exist.

Tone: this is harder to articulate. Some statements feel like the venue is showing me their work. Some feel like the venue is presenting me with a fait accompli. The first builds trust, the second erodes it.

**Anil:** Last question — how could a software tool change settlement from your side?

**Sarah:** A few ways. One, if the deal terms were structured and we both could see the same version of them, that'd eliminate a class of disputes that come from reading ambiguity. Two, if the settlement statement showed its work — every line traceable, every assumption visible — that'd change the trust dynamic. Three, if I could see the settlement before my tour manager signed off on it — like, get a preview while they're at the table — I'd be a faster signoff and there'd be fewer next-day disputes.

The big shift would be: settlement becomes a structured collaboration between the venue and the agency, not an asymmetric document the venue sends to the agency the next day. Right now it's a black box that I open in the morning.

**Anil:** Useful. Thank you Sarah.

**Sarah:** Sure thing. Tell Mariana I said hi. She's one of the good ones.
